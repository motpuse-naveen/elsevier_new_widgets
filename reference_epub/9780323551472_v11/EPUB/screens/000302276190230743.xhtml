DOS AND DON’TS OF TAKING MEDICINES

Select DO or DON’T for the following scenarios.

Multiple Choice Question Group

Question 1

 1. If your medicine prescription label says: 1 tab PO a.c., you take it before meals.

Feedback

Answer:  DO (a.c. means ante cibum “before meals”. Some medications are better absorbed on an empty stomach, such as 1 hour before eating or 2 hours after eating. READ labels carefully!)

Question 1

2. If your medicine prescription label says: 1 PO qPM prn pain, you take it every morning.

Feedback

Answer:  DON’T (qPM means “every evening” and PRN means “when needed.”)

Multiple Choice Question Group

Question 1

3. If you feel 100% better, skip the last few doses of antibiotic medication.   

Feedback

Answer: DON’T (Antibiotics work best when an optimal blood level of the drug is reached and then maintained for a period of time. If you stop your course of treatment early, you may prevent complete eradication of infection. Finish your entire course of medication and call your doctor if you are not feeling better.)

Feedback

Multiple Choice Question Group

Question 1

4. Lend a friend your pills if she has the same condition or is taking the same medication. 

Feedback

Answer: DON’T (People take different doses of medications and for different reasons. NEVER SHARE. Any mistake could have serious side effects.)

Multiple Choice Question Group

Question 1

5. You may take your cholesterol-lowering statin pill with grapefruit juice.   

Feedback

Answer: DON’T (Grapefruit juice increases blood levels of Lipitor that may lead to toxic side effects such as liver and muscle damage.)

Feedback

Multiple Choice Question Group

Question 1

6. It’s a good idea to take your ACE inhibitor (blood pressure medication) with bananas.

Feedback

Answer: DON’T (Bananas contain potassium, and ACE inhibitors cause the body to retain potassium. If you combine the two, you may end up with a dangerously high level of potassium in the blood.)

Feedback

Multiple Choice Question Group

Question 1

7. Add vegetables like kale, mustard greens, and spinach to your diet when you are taking the anticoagulant warfarin (Coumadin).

Feedback

Answer: DON’T (Kale and similar vegetables are rich in vitamin K. Warfarin works as an anticoagulant by decreasing the activity of vitamin K, which is necessary for clotting. Therefore, kale and other vegetables can counteract the anti-clotting beneﬁts of warfarin.)

Feedback

Multiple Choice Question Group

Question 1

8. After taking your heart medicine digoxin, you may enjoy a treat of black licorice.

Feedback

Answer: DON’T (Black licorice, or glycyrrhiza glabra, decreases potassium levels in the body. Low potassium levels can increase the side effects that may occur with digoxin usage. These side effects are nausea, vomiting, headache, loss of appetite and diarrhea.)

Feedback

Multiple Choice Question Group

Question 1

9. Enjoy a glass or two of wine when you are taking the antibiotic Flagyl (metronidazole).

Feedback

Answer: DON’T (Flagyl interferes with how alcohol is eliminated from the body and results in gastrointestinal symptoms like nausea and vomiting.)

Feedback

Answer: DON’T (Walnuts, Brazil nuts, soybean ﬂ our, and high-ﬁ ber foods can prevent your body from absorbing thyroid medication.)

PRESCRIPTIONS

The usual order of drug prescription information is as follows: name of the drug, dosage, route of administration, timing of administration. Frequently, the physician will include a qualifying phrase to indicate why the prescription is being written. Not all information is listed with every prescription.

CONTROLLED SUBSTANCES

Controlled substances are drugs regulated under existing federal law. The substances are divided into five classes (schedules) based on the substance’s medicinal value, harmfulness, and potential for abuse or addiction. Schedule I includes the most dangerous drugs that have no recognized medicinal use, and Schedule V includes the least dangerous drugs.
